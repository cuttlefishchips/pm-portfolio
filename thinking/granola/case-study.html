01  ·  Problem

Granola stops at recall. The real value is what happens after.

Granola has solved capture. Commitments stay buried in individual notes, organised by when the meeting happened, not by what still needs doing.

For users in 4–7 meetings daily, this means maintaining a parallel task system outside Granola, always slightly out of date. Granola's co-founder has stated the ambition: "It is not about just capturing a meeting, but a space where you can work, even post meetings."

The gap

The current loop stops at Recall: Meet → Capture → Recall. Open Items closes it by adding Act. The feature exists in the transcript. It just hasn't been surfaced yet.

02  ·  Key Decisions

Three choices that shaped the feature

Three decisions where I chose a direction and rejected an alternative. Each one reflects a judgment about where feature trust comes from and what breaks it.

01

Precision over recall in extraction: conservative by default

The alternative

Broad extraction with user-side filtering. Surface everything, let users cull the noise. Maximises recall on day one.

The call

If the first list contains noise, users conclude the feature is broken and don't return. Better to surface 6 high-confidence items than 12 that include noise. Completion rate within a fixed timeframe is also excluded: a 2-hour action and a 2-week follow-up are different kinds of items, and a fixed window incentivises the wrong extractions.

02

60-day full trial, then paid only: no permanent free tier

The alternative

A permanent free tier with caps. Builds a larger user base. Alternatively, a short capped preview to create an early conversion moment.

The call

A permanent free tier subsidises non-converters and spends the pricing lever early. A capped preview creates friction at peak engagement. A full trial avoids both: the user accumulates 60 days of evidence before a conversion decision. Expiry is framed around items and history at risk, not feature marketing.

03

Open Items must never require user input to function

The alternative

Mandatory priority fields, required labelling, AI-inferred due dates. More structured output. Closer to how most task tools work.

The call

Every addition must be additive, never required. If a user never touches a configuration option, Open Items must still be fully useful. Once users fill in fields to make the list work, it becomes a worse version of every task manager they already use.

03  ·  Solution

Open Items

A native Granola view that extracts personal commitments from meeting notes and surfaces them in one consolidated list, with source context. No setup. No configuration.

Each item carries the action text, a context snippet from the transcript, the source meeting and date, and a status: Open, Done, or Dismissed. Done and Dismissed are intentionally distinct: completing a task and deciding it's no longer relevant are different signals.

Vision

Transform Granola from a place where your meetings live into a system that ensures your meetings actually lead to something.

Phase 1 · MVP

Personal

  • Auto-extraction, cross-meeting view
  • Done and Dismissed states
  • Context snippets and source links
  • Sidebar badge for new items
  • 60-day trial, then paid
Phase 2 · Growth

Team

  • Team view across shared folders
  • AI-inferred priority (opt-in)
  • Smart grouping by theme
  • Custom labels and tags
  • Item assignment to teammates
Phase 3 · Vision

Enterprise

  • Open Items in People and Companies views
  • Commitments by contact or deal
  • Export to Linear, Notion, Asana
  • System of record across the org
04  ·  Success Metrics

Measuring list trust, not list completion

The north star is whether Open Items becomes part of the daily workflow. Completion rate within a fixed timeframe is explicitly not a metric: a 2-hour action and a 2-week follow-up are different kinds of items, and a fixed window rewards short-horizon items over long ones.

North Star

Weekly Active Open Items Users / Weekly Active Granola Users. Whether the feature is part of the core workflow, not tried once and abandoned.

<15% Immediate Dismissal Rate
20% Session Entry via Open Items (90 days)
Exceed Trial-to-Paid vs. Existing Benchmark

Immediate Dismissal Rate measures items dismissed within 60 seconds. It's the strongest passive proxy for extraction noise, and the one metric that signals a feature-ending problem before it compounds.

Session Entry via Open Items is the habit signal: the share of daily active users who open Granola through Open Items first. Once it's load-bearing, the number moves.

Trial-to-Paid Conversion is benchmarked against Granola's existing conversion rates, not an absolute target. The feature has to pull its own weight in the monetisation model.

05  ·  Tradeoffs

What we gave up, and why

Two tradeoffs that shaped the launch conditions.

01

Feature gravity locks the pricing decision

The constraint

Pricing decisions can usually be revisited as a feature matures. The instinct is to launch and adjust.

The reality

Once placed in the paid plan, the feature can't migrate to a higher tier. The launch decision is also the pricing ceiling decision. This had to be right at the start, not corrected later.

02

Extraction quality is the only day-one kill switch

The constraint

Most feature risks surface gradually after launch, leaving room to iterate. The usual instinct is to ship and learn.

The reality

Extraction quality kills the feature on first impression. There is no recovery window. Human-rated quality checks are required before launch, not after, and the bar for acceptable noise is set by the dismissal rate target, not internal judgment.

06  ·  Reflection

The question that stays open

Open Items is a hypothetical extension, not a shipped feature. What the exercise clarified is that rule is harder to hold in practice than it is to state. Every Phase 2 addition (team sharing, assignment, labels) puts pressure on that constraint. The design question for any future build is not what to add, but what to refuse.

What I'd do differently

The hypothetical framing made it easy to hold to that rule. In a real build, there would be pressure to add team features earlier. I'd want a formal constraint review at the start of every phase, not only at the feature level, to make the tradeoff explicit before it's already locked in.
