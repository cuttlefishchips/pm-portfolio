01  ·  Context

Singapore is aging. Families are the ones absorbing the load.

Singapore is projected to be a super-aged society by 2026. Families are already absorbing the load. Coordinating care across family members, domestic workers, healthcare providers, and government programmes falls on whoever is closest.

210K+Caregivers in Singapore
75%Feel tired and overwhelmed
1 in 3Feel unsupported
Signal

"Dad had a physio appointment Tuesday. None of us knew who was taking him. We found out when the clinic called to say he didn't show."

That story came up repeatedly. Not a resource problem. A coordination problem.

02  ·  Ecosystem Research

We mapped every existing solution. They all solved the wrong problem.

Product Market Type Gap
Homage Singapore Marketplace Matches families with care professionals. Doesn't help families already doing the care.
AIC eCare Singapore Info / Navigation Helps navigate government services. No workflow support.
CareCompass.sg Singapore Info / Navigation Resource directory. Passive. Doesn't help with daily coordination.
SG Assist Singapore Info / Navigation Community forum. Unstructured. No accountability layer.
Lotsa Helping Hands Global Coordination Closest model to what we needed. US-centric. Weak on professional integration.
Cura Systems Global Marketplace Professional care management. Not designed for family-led care.

Every existing solution assumes the problem is finding care. But most families already have care in place. The problem is that nobody knows what everyone else is doing.

03  ·  The Insight

The problem isn't finding care. It's coordinating it.

A care marketplace serves families who don't yet have a plan. We built for families already in the middle of it, doing the care in fragments.

vs

Care marketplace vs. coordination infrastructure

What everyone else built

A care marketplace. Primary user: families searching for services. Core loop: discover → book → pay. Success metric: transactions. Problem: most families already have care in place.

What we built

Coordination infrastructure. Primary user: families already providing care. Core loop: assign → track → update. Success metric: coordination clarity. Problem: the relay is broken, not the roster.

Caregiving is a relay. Tag Team needed to make the handoff feel like a system, not a prayer.

04  ·  Tag Team

Six features built on one reframe.

The prototype was built and shipped during the hackathon. Every feature follows directly from the core insight: the problem is coordination, not access. Six things families with care in place actually need. View prototype ↗

01

Shared Timeline

Central feed for updates, tasks, and care events. The single source of truth the group chat never was. Everything visible to everyone on the care team.

02

Task + Event Logging

Add care needs without micromanaging. Tasks attach to people, not conversations. Appointments, medications, therapy. One place, not five threads.

03

Care Log

Track condition and behavior over time. Structured notes that don't disappear into a chat thread. Templates guide what gets logged so nothing important gets missed.

04

Voice & Photo Input

Easy updates for helpers or elderly users who can't type. Reduces the barrier to logging care where it matters most: at the point of care, not after.

05

Reactions & Comments

Show support on posts. Coordination without abandoning the human side. The care team stays emotionally present, not just operationally informed.

06

Notifications

Ensures everyone has a common understanding. The thing that makes the relay feel like a system. No one finds out about a missed appointment from the clinic.

User feedback

"TagTeam makes it easy to see upcoming tasks, update my care group and stay organised. Took the mental load off caregiving." — T03, 45F, caring for 68F mother

05  ·  Reflection

Although we didn't place, the framing reached the right people.

Several agencies reached out after the event. Not because of the prototype, but because the problem framing was recognised as correct by people who work in eldercare. That's a different kind of validation than a hackathon result.

The coordination infrastructure concept is in active discussion. The service navigation component remains the open question: it risks reintroducing the marketplace logic the framing argues against.

What I'd do differently

The service navigation feature is critical for adoption but architecturally in tension with the core thesis. That trade-off deserved a cleaner resolution before the prototype. Not after.
