A reference for writing portfolio copy that stays true to the design system and to the person behind it. Every visual token has a voice equivalent. This card maps both.

The brand position

Five years building products where a physical object and a digital layer have to work together. Most of what I know about product thinking came from getting that boundary wrong a few times first.

What this sentence is doing

Fast-context builder, not generalist. Physical-digital background named as the source of the thinking, not as a job title. Humility built in: the learning came from getting it wrong.

01 · Three tonal registers, one per act

Act 1 · Mighty Jaxx

Professional authority

Clear about what was decided and why. Specific about outcomes. Not self-aggrandising, but not vague either. The work is real, and the writing treats it that way.

"The data showed most vault additions happened before the first scroll, so we moved the CTA up. Wishlist additions went up 18% over the following two weeks." Specific · Evidence-first · Outcome named · No ego
Act 2 · Self-Initiated

Intellectual curiosity

Honest about starting from scratch. Shows the curiosity that drove it. Names what was found without overstating it. The voice of someone who went somewhere unfamiliar and came back with genuine questions.

"I didn't work in fintech. Going in without assumptions helped me see something the product had been quietly papering over for a while." Honest · Curious · Reframe without the swagger
Act 3 · Pulse

Builder's warmth

Warm and a little personal. Shows genuine curiosity about the problem. Honest about what worked and what didn't. Lets the product speak for itself rather than overselling it.

"It started because I was opening four different apps to understand one run. I wanted something that connected all of it and just told me what mattered." Personal · Origin story · Problem-first · No hype

02 · Before and after · Translating portfolio-speak into Reikotan

Portfolio-speak · Do not use

I am a product manager with a passion for user-centred design.

Collaborated cross-functionally to drive alignment on key product decisions.

Leveraged data insights to improve key metrics across the platform.

I have a proven track record of delivering results in ambiguous environments.

This project taught me the importance of stakeholder communication.

I pick up context fast. That's not a soft skill.

Reikotan voice · Use this

Six weeks into a domain I'd never worked in, I found something the team had been puzzling over for months. Going in without assumptions helped.

There was a disagreement about whether to build the feature at all. We decided not to. This is what led us there.

Wishlist additions went up 18% after one change to image sizing. The signal was in the data for a while before anyone ran the test.

The brief asked us to improve the flow. What we found was that the feature people wanted wasn't there yet. So that became the brief.

The first two versions didn't work. The third one did, and understanding why the earlier ones failed was what made the difference.

I got curious about fintech even though it's not my day-to-day. I wanted to see if the thinking transferred. It did, mostly, and where it didn't was interesting too.

03 · Rules and qualities · Non-negotiable

Humility is structural.

Never position yourself against others. Never imply you saw what the team missed or brought something they lacked. The work speaks for itself. If a line only works because it implies someone else was wrong or slower, rewrite it.

Elegant and gracious.

The writing should feel considered, not clipped. Warm without being effusive. Every sentence earns its place. Gracefulness comes from giving the reader enough without giving them too much.

Length in balance.

Short sentences create emphasis. Longer ones carry texture and thought. The two should work together. Vary deliberately, not by accident.

01

Start with what happened.

Lead with the situation or finding, not a claim about yourself. Let the reader draw the conclusion. Fraunces rewards the short, grounded sentence. Evidence is more convincing than self-description.

✕ "Throughout this project I had the opportunity to explore various approaches to the problem of user retention."
→ "Retention had been declining for two quarters. Week two of the project is when we found out why."
02

Show the decision, not just the output.

Outputs are what shipped. Decisions are what you thought through. A portfolio that only shows outputs looks like a design portfolio. Showing the reasoning is what makes it a PM portfolio.

✕ "We launched a new onboarding flow with improved UX and reduced drop-off."
→ "We cut onboarding from seven steps to three. It was a contentious call. This is what the data said and how we got there."
03

Be specific or be silent.

Numbers, timeframes, names of things. "Improved key metrics" says nothing. "62% of vault additions happened before the first scroll" says everything. DM Mono is there to carry exactly these kinds of specifics.

✕ "Drove significant improvements in user engagement across the platform."
→ "+18% wishlist additions. One change. Image size. Six weeks of data."
04

Name the reframe when it happened.

Some of the most interesting PM work is realising the problem you started with wasn't quite the right one. When that happened, say so. It's not a mistake to admit. It's the whole point.

✕ "After conducting user research I identified several opportunities for improvement."
→ "The brief was to improve the flow. A few weeks in, we realised the issue was upstream of that. So the brief changed."
05

Write like a person, not a style guide.

No em-dashes. Replace with a period, a colon, or rewrite the sentence. Vary sentence length deliberately. Short sentences land harder after long ones. Long sentences carry texture when the thought needs it. Read copy aloud before publishing. If you wouldn't say it that way, rewrite it.

✕ "I leveraged cross-functional alignment to drive — and deliver — a 56% improvement in authenticated user identification."
→ "14,381 users scanned and came through as identified collectors. Before QR, they were anonymous buyers."

07 · Content-type guidance · Where copy slips

Hero copy / writing about yourself

What goes wrong: adjectives replace evidence. You describe yourself instead of showing yourself.

Do not use

"I'm a product manager with a background in physical-digital products and a track record of shipping in ambiguous environments."

Use this

"Five years building products where a physical object and a digital layer have to work together. The problems are different from pure software, and so is the thinking that comes out of it."

Case study opening paragraph

What goes wrong: first paragraph is background, not story. Company and role before problem.

Do not use

"At Mighty Jaxx, I led the authentication team responsible for transitioning our product line from NFC to QR-based verification."

Use this

"The NFC chip cost ten times what a QR code costs. That number sat underneath a lot of decisions for longer than it should have."

Role and contribution

What goes wrong: verbs describe involvement, not decisions. Led. Collaborated. Was responsible for.

Do not use

"I led cross-functional collaboration between engineering, design, and operations to deliver the QR migration."

Use this

"Engineering needed to trust the rollout plan before anything moved. Getting there took longer than the timeline assumed, and the conversations along the way changed what we shipped."

Metrics and results

What goes wrong: the number is there but the framing is flat. The metric lands without weight.

Do not use

"The QR migration resulted in a 56% repeat scan rate and 14,381 identified users."

Use this

"14,381 users scanned and came through as identified collectors. Before QR, they were anonymous buyers. That distinction matters for everything that comes after the sale."

08 · Copy self-review · Run before publishing

01

Does the opening line start with what happened, not with who you are?

02

Is there a decision visible, not just an output?

03

Is every metric framed, not just stated?

04

Are there any em-dashes? Replace them.

05

Any words from the swap list? leveraged · collaborated · drove alignment · key metrics · passionate about

06

Read it aloud. Does any sentence sound like a LinkedIn bio? Rewrite it.

07

Could this copy describe any PM, or does it only work for you?

08

Does any line imply you found what others missed, or position you as smarter than the room? Rewrite it.

09 · Application notes · How the rules work in practice

These notes came out of live editing sessions. They clarify where the rules above were being applied too strictly, and where nuance matters.

Transitions are not filler.

Words like "however", "as it turns out", and "on the other hand" are connective tissue, not hedging. Cutting every transition makes copy rigid and exhausting. Preserve them when they create flow between ideas. The filler rule targets openers that delay the point — not words that link sentences.

Sharpness needs breathing room.

Three consecutive short sentences feel staccato, not punchy. Vary length deliberately — a longer sentence carrying texture, followed by a short one for emphasis. The goal is rhythm, not brevity for its own sake. If reading aloud feels choppy, add a connective or extend a sentence.

Humility is about others, not yourself.

The humility rule means: never position yourself against the team, never imply you saw what others missed. It does not mean you can't claim your work. Stating what you decided is fine. What's not fine is claiming it twice — if you've already listed the decisions, "the product decisions were mine" at the end is redundant and reads as defensive.

Where the rules apply hardest

These are non-negotiable regardless of context.

Always fix

Em-dashes · swap list words (leveraged, drove, primary driver, cross-functional alignment) · vague metrics ("very low", "significant improvement") · ownership stated twice · opening lines that start with your role rather than the situation

Where judgment applies

Apply the spirit of the rule, not the letter.

Use judgment

Transitions and connective tissue · sentence length (vary, don't always shorten) · colons (useful for introducing lists; overuse makes a document feel like a series of definitions) · ownership language (one clear statement of what you decided is right; repetition is not)
