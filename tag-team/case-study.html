01  ·  Context

Singapore is aging. Families are the ones absorbing the load.

Singapore is projected to be a super-aged society by 2026, with one in five residents over 65. Families are already in it: juggling careers, young children, and daily care coordination across family members, domestic workers, healthcare providers, and government programmes.

"Dad had a physio appointment Tuesday. None of us knew who was taking him. We found out when the clinic called to say he didn't show."

That story came up repeatedly. Not a resource problem. A coordination problem.

02  ·  Ecosystem Research

We mapped every existing solution. They all solved the wrong problem.

Product Market Type Gap
Homage Singapore Marketplace Matches families with care professionals. Doesn't help families already doing the care.
AIC eCare Singapore Info / Navigation Helps navigate government services. No workflow support.
CareCompass.sg Singapore Info / Navigation Resource directory. Passive. Doesn't help with daily coordination.
SG Assist Singapore Info / Navigation Community forum. Unstructured. No accountability layer.
Lotsa Helping Hands Global Coordination Closest model to what we needed. US-centric. Weak on professional integration.
Cura Systems Global Marketplace Professional care management. Not designed for family-led care.

Every existing solution assumes the problem is finding care. But most families already have care in place. The problem is that nobody knows what everyone else is doing.

03  ·  The Insight

The problem isn't finding care. It's coordinating it.

A care marketplace serves families who don't yet have a plan. We built for families already in the middle of it, doing the care in fragments.

What everyone else built
A care marketplace
Primary user: families searching for services. Core loop: discover → book → pay. Success metric: transactions. Problem: most families already have care in place.
What we built
Coordination infrastructure
Primary user: families already providing care. Core loop: assign → track → update. Success metric: coordination clarity. Problem: the relay is broken, not the roster.

Caregiving is a relay. Tag Team needed to make the handoff feel like a system, not a prayer.

04  ·  Tag Team

Four workflows. One shared view of care.

01
Shared Responsibility
A central task board visible to the full care team: family, helpers, professionals. Who does what, when, and whether it's done. No more tracking across WhatsApp threads.
02
Care Timeline
One shared calendar for appointments, medications, therapy, and daily needs. One view instead of five.
03
Structured Updates
A structured log replacing group chats: medication given, doctor notes, auto-notifications. Searchable, not buried in conversation.
04
Service Navigation
Singapore has extensive support programmes, but most families don't know what they qualify for. Tag Team surfaces relevant schemes based on the care situation.
05  ·  Reflection

We didn't win. The idea didn't stop there.

The team didn't place. A coordination platform is harder to demo in 48 hours than a tool with immediate visible output. But several agencies reached out after the event, and we are in active discussions. People who work in this space every day recognised the problem framing.

Post-hackathon agency interest
Multiple agencies reached out. Currently in active discussions. The problem framing landed with domain experts, not just hackathon judges.
What I'd do differently
Four workflows is the right long-term shape, wrong starting point. The shared responsibility board should have been the entire v1. The rest follows once families are anchored into a coordination habit.
The open question
The service navigation component is valuable but risky. Maintaining accurate programme data across multiple agencies is an operational challenge as much as a product one.
