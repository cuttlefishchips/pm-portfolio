01  ·  Context

A company selling millions of products to people it couldn't see

Mighty Jaxx sells collectible designer toys globally through distributors and retailers. The channel works at scale but leaves no visibility into who actually owns the products.

QR Auth created the authentication layer; Vault is the engagement platform built on top of it. To get both live, I worked with engineering, design, brand, marketing, production, and licensing across every stage.

My ownership: product strategy, prioritisation, and sequencing. I framed the problem, scoped the first shipped version, and decided what launched and what was deferred.

02  ·  Discovery

Four signals, same root cause

Internal data gap. Marketing couldn't activate collectors because collectors didn't exist in any system.

NFC authentication was failing silently. An NFC flow existed but completion rates were low enough that the flow was effectively broken. Reader location, manual activation, and positioning requirements blocked users in ways we couldn't see.

Community demand for authenticity, no official solution. Collector forums were active with counterfeit discussions and no official solution simple enough to actually use.

Marketing and licensing flagging the same dead end. Both teams flagged the same thing independently: once a product sells through the channel, the relationship ends.

The framing trap

Early internal pressure framed this as an anti-counterfeiting problem: build a verify-and-done scanner, close the counterfeit gap. However, that framing was too narrow. A verify-only system has no reason to be opened twice. The real problem was identity: the brand had no ongoing relationship with the people who owned its products. Counterfeiting was a symptom of the same gap.

Root cause

These weren't four separate problems. They were four symptoms of a single missing layer: collector identity. As it turns out, the brand knew its distributors, but not its collectors.

03  ·  The Insight

Identity before loyalty

The first product wasn't Vault. It was the identity layer underneath it: scan once, exist in the system.

Authentication and ownership registration had to happen in one action. QR over NFC was the right call, because it eliminated friction users couldn't resolve on their own. NFC tags were invisible, reader location was unclear, and some devices needed manual activation. On the other hand, QR needs no explanation.

04  ·  Product Decisions

Three decisions that shaped the system

01

NFC to QR: a behaviour decision, not a technology decision

Before: NFC

NFC tags were embedded and invisible. Users couldn't find the reader, some devices needed manual activation, and completion rates were low enough that the flow was effectively broken.

After: QR

QR needs no explanation. The reason wasn't cost. It was friction users couldn't resolve on their own.

02

Authentication as engagement gateway, not anti-counterfeiting tool

Initial framing

Some stakeholders saw scanning as verify-and-done. Engagement features felt like scope creep on a contained infrastructure problem.

My position

Scanning had to be the entry point: ownership registration, collector identity, campaign access. Once you've scanned, there's somewhere to go.

03

Campaigns before loyalty tiers: sequence matters

What we deferred

Tiered loyalty, social profiles, and secondary market transfer were all on the table. Top-collector tiers and social features needed engagement history first. Shipping them before anyone had scanned anything would have been structure without substance.

The sequence

Campaign-based engagement shipped first: time-bound missions, milestone unlocks, IP-specific challenges. Once the behaviour loop was established, a loyalty tier for the most active collectors followed. Private drops, early access, built on real scan data rather than assumptions.

05  ·  Outcomes

Foundation for loyalty, not the finished system

Mighty Jaxx can now identify who its collectors are, which did not exist before.

0→1 Collector identity layer QR Auth created something that didn't previously exist. A growing, queryable database of collectors tied to specific products and IPs, built entirely through post-purchase behaviour.
QR Friction removed by design NFC required knowing where to tap, manual activation on some devices, and precise positioning, while QR requires none of that. The switch meant collectors could scan straight from the packaging with any phone camera, no setup or instruction needed.
✓ Loop validated With the success of the IP campaign pilot, the core behaviour was validated. Collectors scan, register, and return when scanning is tied to real progression.

Marketing and licensing unblocked. Both teams can now segment and activate collectors. IP-specific targeting, previously impossible, became a standard campaign input.

Infrastructure for what comes next. Given the identity layer QR Auth created, marketing and licensing could now build loyalty tiers for top collectors, run campaign mechanics, and target by IP, all on real collector data.

06  ·  Since launch

The loop works. The between-launches problem remains.

QR Auth created a collector identity layer that didn't previously exist. For a brand that knew only bulk distributor volumes, that shift is structural, not incremental.

An IP campaign pilot confirmed the core loop: scan, register, unlock. Collectors engage when scanning is tied to real progression. What the campaign also revealed is the next constraint. The platform currently has the strongest reason to open at launch, when the scan moment happens. Between product waves, there's no natural pull.

That's the problem the next phase is designed to test. If IP-based campaigns can create habitual opens between launches, through time-limited missions, IP-specific content, and loyalty progression, then Vault becomes a retention surface, not just an onboarding one. The first structured IP campaign is the live test of that thesis.
