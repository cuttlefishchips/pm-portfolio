01  ·  Context

Why a collectibles company moved into interactive toys

Mighty Jaxx built a strong business around collectible designer figures — one-time purchases that end when they go on a shelf. IP partners wanted fan engagement that extended beyond purchase. The brief was a screen-free interactive toy: a physical object that talks back, without a display, without buttons, without setup.

I was PM across interaction design, content ecosystem structure, hardware-software behaviour definition, and licensing partner coordination.

02  ·  The Audience

Designing for two decision-makers with different goals

The product has two decision-makers with opposing priorities. Every major design decision navigated both.

Primary user

The child

Characters they love. Stories that respond. Interaction that feels immediate and alive, not scripted.

Decision-maker

The parent

No screen. Age-appropriate content. Visibility into what the product says to their child.

Key tension

Solving for both meant no decision was clean. Every feature trade-off had to be made explicit — features that delighted children often made parents nervous, and vice versa.

03  ·  Ecosystem Design

One experience, four connected components

A standalone speaker lacks physical interaction; physical toys alone lack storytelling. The product had to be an ecosystem: hardware, content, and software as a unified experience.

Ecosystem diagram — speaker base · figurines · content system · parent app

Figurines: NFC-enabled characters with ~60 minutes of base content — stories, songs, interactive experiences, and a distinct personality shaping conversational responses.

Playsets: Environment accessories (forest, home, school) that introduce new activities. Character-playset pairings unlock bonus content, creating cross-sell incentive.

Speaker base: Manages NFC detection, voice I/O, LED cues, and mode transitions. No screen.

Content system: Pre-loaded stories work offline; branching narratives and conversational mode require a connection. New characters and content expand the system over time.

4 system components
~60 minutes per character
0 buttons or screens

Ecosystem diagrams were the most useful artefact for aligning engineering, content, and IP partners on scope. A single diagram replaced three separate briefing documents.

04  ·  Product Decisions

Three decisions that shaped the system

Each involved real constraints and competing pressures — not obvious calls at the time.

01

Committing to screen-free, even when a screen would have been easier

The temptation

Early concepts included a small display on the base. A screen would have made it easier to show animations, guide children through prompts, and give demos something visible. Internal teams liked the option.

Why we removed it

A screen puts the product in direct competition with tablets, collapsing the value proposition. Without it, the interaction simplified: place a character, respond to a voice.

02

NFC as the physical trigger: choosing simplicity over capability

Alternatives considered

Bluetooth required pairing and batteries in each figurine. Camera recognition raised privacy concerns and had lighting reliability issues.

Why NFC

NFC identifies a figurine the instant it touches the base: no pairing, no batteries in the toy. Place the character, it activates. Any setup step breaks the interaction for a 3–10 year old.

What I'd do differently

Test NFC detection reliability across figurine materials and sizes earlier. We found edge cases late in hardware validation that required a minor base redesign.

03

How open should character conversations be? A real design debate.

Option A: Fully open

Characters respond to almost any question within safety limits. Natural, but conversations drift outside the character's universe, removing the incentive to buy new playsets.

Option B: Scene-locked

Conversations tightly constrained by the active playset. Children become frustrated by constant redirects, and the interaction feels scripted rather than alive.

What we chose: Hybrid

Characters respond broadly, but answers are shaped by the active character, environment, and safety layer. Off-scene questions get acknowledged, then redirected: "That's interesting! But since we're in the forest today, want to explore the river with me?"

05  ·  Designing Under Constraints

The product is defined by what it can't do

Screen-free interaction means the system communicates only through voice, sound, and light. Hardware minimalism means a single NFC sensor, microphones, and LED indicators. Designing within those limits was the brief.

No screen. Navigation became placement. Menus became voice prompts. Visual feedback became LED cues and sound design.

Single NFC sensor, limited inputs. One figurine at a time, no physical buttons beyond power. The entire interaction model is built around placement and voice — the two most natural inputs for young children.

Children's unpredictable responses. Children pause, repeat phrases, answer unrelated questions, go silent, or talk over the character. The system handles all of these with fallback behaviours that keep stories moving without making the child feel like they did something wrong.

Licensed IP character integrity. Licensed characters have tightly controlled tone, vocabulary, and worldview. A conversational system that generates flexible responses is in direct tension with that. A single out-of-character response in a live partner demo could end a licensing relationship. A policy layer, character personality guidelines, and I/O filtering are built into the system from the start.

Safety requirements for direct child interaction. Input filtering, output validation, and conversation boundaries enforced by a policy engine — all running before any response reaches the child.

06  ·  Pre-Launch Validation

Validating interaction assumptions before the full build

The product is still in development. Research focused on the core interaction assumptions that had to be true for the ecosystem to work at all.

01

Will children understand the interaction model without instruction?

The risk

If children couldn't figure out that placing a figurine activates the character, nothing else would work.

Signals we looked for

Children attempting to place the figurine without being told. Understanding that the character was now "active" and listening. Responding to voice prompts without coaching.

02

How do children actually behave during voice interaction?

What we assumed

Children would respond on cue, stay on topic, and progress through story segments predictably.

What we observed

Long pauses before responding (3–5+ seconds), repeating prompts back instead of answering, unrelated questions, talking over the character mid-sentence. These patterns directly shaped the fallback response design: stories continue regardless of how the child responds.

03

Do children form emotional connection with character voices?

Why it matters

Without emotional connection to the character, there is no reason to return. Return is the business model.

Signals we looked for

Continuing conversations beyond the first prompt unprompted. Asking questions back to the character. Replaying story segments voluntarily. Referring to the character by name.

07  ·  Cross-Functional Execution

Four workstreams that couldn't proceed independently

Hardware, content, licensing, and partner demos each owned a critical piece. The hardest cross-functional dependency was licensed character integrity: a single out-of-character response in a live partner demo could end a licensing relationship, which made the policy layer and I/O filtering non-negotiable from the start.

What this required

Weekly syncs across hardware, content, and licensing to surface dependencies early. The policy engine had to be defined before content scripting could begin, and content structure had to be defined before hardware audio specs were finalised.

08  ·  What remains open

The hardware model is solved. The content pipeline isn't.

Pre-launch validation confirmed that children understand the placement interaction without instruction, and that fallback response design is not a polish decision — it is what makes the product safe to ship.

The unresolved question is not hardware. The hardware interaction model is defined and testable. What isn't yet resolved is whether the content pipeline can scale fast enough to sustain engagement past the first few characters.

Open to work.
