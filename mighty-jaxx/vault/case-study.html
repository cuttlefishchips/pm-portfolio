Context

A company selling millions of products to people it couldn't see

Mighty Jaxx sells collectible designer toys globally through distributors and retailers. The channel works at scale but leaves no visibility into who actually owns the products. I defined and shipped the authentication infrastructure (QR Auth) and the collector engagement platform built on top of it (Vault), across engineering, design, brand, marketing, production, and licensing.

Discovery

Four signals, same root cause

01
Internal data gap

Marketing couldn't activate collectors because collectors didn't exist in any system.

02
NFC authentication was failing silently

An NFC flow existed but completion rates were very low. Reader location, manual activation, and positioning requirements blocked users in ways we couldn't see.

03
Community demand for authenticity, no official solution

Collector forums were active with counterfeit discussions and no official verification mechanism simple enough to use at scale.

04
Marketing and licensing flagging the same dead end

Marketing and licensing flagged the same thing independently: once a product sells through the channel, the relationship ends.

The Insight

Identity before loyalty

The first product wasn't Vault. It was the identity layer underneath it: scan once, exist in the system.

Product Decisions

Three decisions that shaped the system

01

NFC to QR: a behaviour decision, not a technology decision

Before: NFC

NFC tags were embedded and invisible. Users couldn't find the reader, some devices needed manual activation, and completion rates were very low.

After: QR

QR needs no explanation. The primary driver wasn't cost — it was eliminating friction users couldn't resolve on their own.

02

Authentication as engagement gateway, not anti-counterfeiting tool

Initial framing

Some stakeholders saw scanning as verify-and-done. Engagement features felt like scope creep on a contained infrastructure problem.

My position

I wanted scanning to be the entry point: ownership registration, collector identity, campaign access. A verify-only system has no reason to be opened twice.

How alignment happened

Alignment came from reframing: not stopping fakes, but building collector relationships. That connected directly to what marketing and licensing were already asking for.

03

Campaigns before loyalty tiers: sequence matters

What we cut

Tiered loyalty, social profiles, and secondary market transfer were all on the table. All were premature.

What we shipped

Campaign-based engagement: time-bound missions, milestone unlocks, IP-specific challenges. Loyalty tiers need engagement history. Campaigns build it.

Execution

This product had to work in factories before it worked on phones.

A QR scan takes a second. Building the infrastructure behind it meant writing manufacturing specs that production lines had to follow — with tolerances that showed up in the collector's scan experience, not on a dashboard.

Two things nearly broke it. First: factory QC. Sticker placement standards were defined early, but inspection guidelines shipped too late. First-batch inconsistencies forced a QC process redesign mid-cycle, while production was already moving. Second: database sync. Every production batch had to link to the authentication database before leaving the factory. A silent sync failure meant QR codes that scanned as invalid at retail — with no way to fix it post-production. There was no fallback. Building monitoring for that dependency was non-negotiable.

Sticker placement standards

Placement specs per product line, balancing aesthetic integration with scan reliability.

Factory QC integration

Inspection guidelines for factories new to digital product identity. Involved production leads earlier after first-batch issues; ended up with a repeatable process across product lines.

Authentication database sync

Every batch had to link to the authentication database before retail. Designed batch ingestion and sync failure monitoring.

Operations monitoring dashboard

Tracked scan activity, auth errors, and batch status. Gave ops real-time visibility and surfaced early collector behaviour signals.

Reflection

What I know now

Learning

Factory spec decisions are product decisions

Where the sticker sits, what surface it adheres to, whether it survives shelf conditions — these are UX decisions. We treated manufacturing integration as logistics. That framing was the root cause of the QC issue.

Learning

First-scan onboarding is a product problem, not a marketing one

Many collectors weren't aware Vault existed after unboxing. We treated it as a marketing problem. It's a product moment — the 30 seconds after opening the box.

Next

Always-on engagement between campaigns

Between launches, active sessions drop. The platform needs passive mechanics (collection display, social proof) to sustain engagement without a live campaign.

Next

Secondary market ownership transfer

Vault reflects first scan only. Collectors trade; current ownership isn't tracked. Solving transfer would unlock the platform for the secondary market.
