Context

A company selling millions of products to people it couldn't see

Mighty Jaxx sells collectible designer toys globally, predominantly through distributors and retailers. That channel architecture creates a structural blind spot: the company sees bulk sales to intermediaries, but has no visibility into the collectors who actually own the products.

This is not a data problem. It's an identity problem. Without knowing who your collectors are, you can't reward them, re-engage them, or build community around them. The distributor model trades direct reach for scale — and at a certain point, that trade-off limits how far the business can grow.

I was the PM responsible for solving it: defining and shipping the authentication infrastructure (QR Auth) and the collector engagement platform built on top of it (Vault). The project ran across engineering, design, brand, marketing, production, and licensing.

Discovery

Four signals pointing to the same gap

The problem didn't surface from a single research initiative. It came from four independent signals that converged on the same root cause: there was no collector identity layer.

01
Internal data gap

The company could see distributor purchase volumes and direct ecommerce orders, but had no visibility into who owned the majority of products in the market. Marketing couldn't segment or activate collectors because collectors didn't exist in any system. High volume, near-zero buyer data.

02
NFC authentication was failing silently

An NFC-based authentication flow existed but showed very low completion rates. Collectors weren't finishing the scan. The friction was behavioural: knowing where the NFC reader sat on their phone, sometimes manual activation, precise positioning required. Users were abandoning before they understood what they were trying to do.

03
Community demand for authenticity, no official solution

Collector forums and social channels were active with authenticity discussions — identifying counterfeits, comparing packaging details, asking retailers to vouch. Clear demand for a verification system. No official mechanism simple enough to use at scale.

04
Marketing and licensing flagging the same dead end

Independently, marketing and licensing teams raised the post-purchase gap: once a product sells through the channel, the relationship ends. No way to activate IP-specific fanbases, reward loyal collectors, or give licensors anything beyond sales figures. The same problem framed from a different direction.

The Problem

You can't build loyalty on anonymous transactions

"A distributor might buy 10,000 units. We had no idea who actually owned them."

This wasn't a CRM gap. It was a structural consequence of the channel model — and solving it required a product approach, not a data one.

No collector identity. Purchases happened through retailers. Post-purchase, the brand had no record of the end buyer.

No working authenticity system. The NFC flow existed but wasn't completing. Collectors fell back to forum comparisons and retailer reputation — neither reliable at scale.

Broadcast-only marketing. Without knowing who owned what, every campaign was generic. IP-specific fanbase activation was structurally impossible.

Dead post-purchase relationship. Once a toy sold, the interaction ended. For a brand where collecting behaviour is inherently ongoing, this was a material missed opportunity.

Weak licensor data. Licensors increasingly expect fan engagement metrics. Sales numbers alone were insufficient — and competitors were starting to offer more.

The Insight

Identity before loyalty

Loyalty programs are engagement surfaces. You can't build an engagement surface on anonymous transactions.

Before we could reward collectors, we had to know who they were. That meant the first product wasn't Vault — it was the identity layer underneath it. QR auth wasn't just anti-counterfeiting infrastructure. It was the mechanism that made everything else possible.

Once a collector scans, they exist in the system. Once they exist, you can reward them, engage them, and build a relationship. Without the scan, there is nothing to build on.

Product Decisions

Three decisions that shaped the system

Each involved real tradeoffs and internal disagreement. The reasoning behind each reflects the product principles that drove the whole initiative.

01

NFC to QR: a behaviour decision, not a technology decision

Before: NFC

NFC was the original approach. Embedded tags, no visible code, stronger anti-tamper story. It fit a compelling demo.

In practice: users didn't know where the NFC reader was on their phone. Some devices required manual activation. Precise positioning was required. Authentication completion rates were very low — the friction was invisible to us but blocking for users.

After: QR

QR codes don't require explanation. Open camera, point, scan. The behaviour is already ingrained. No app switching, no NFC toggle, no positioning.

Cost and scalability benefits were real but secondary. The primary driver was eliminating friction users couldn't intuitively resolve. A system with 10% adoption doesn't build collector identity — regardless of how clean the technology is.

Optimise for behaviour users already understand, not the technology that impresses internally.
02

Authentication as engagement gateway, not anti-counterfeiting tool

Initial framing

Some stakeholders viewed QR scanning primarily as anti-counterfeiting. Scan, verify authenticity, done. A single-use utility with no reason to return.

The concern was scope: engagement features on top of authentication felt like adding complexity to what should be a contained infrastructure problem.

My position

I pushed to make scanning the entry point into the collector ecosystem — registering ownership, creating identity, unlocking campaigns, initiating progression. Authentication and engagement become the same action.

A single-use auth system would see declining scan rates over time. Once a collector verifies their first toy, the behaviour has nowhere to go. That's not collector identity — it's a one-time receipt.

How alignment happened

The pivot came from reframing the business question. The goal wasn't to stop counterfeits (real but bounded) — it was to build direct relationships with collectors. Anti-counterfeiting alone doesn't do that. Scan-as-entry-point does. Connecting this to marketing and licensing's stated post-purchase problem made it concrete: they needed collector identity to do their jobs. The product decision became the solution to an org-wide problem, not a PM preference.

The scan had to do double duty — verify authenticity and activate engagement — or neither would achieve meaningful adoption.
03

Campaigns before loyalty tiers: sequence matters

What we cut

Early thinking included tiered loyalty levels tied to cumulative behaviour, social profiles, friend networks, and secondary market ownership transfer.

Each was real and valuable. All were premature.

What we shipped

Campaign-based engagement: time-bound missions, milestone unlocks, IP-specific challenges. Simpler to build, faster to validate, directly tied to product launches and licensing cycles.

Loyalty tiers require engagement history to draw on. Campaigns generate that history first. Shipping tiers before understanding how collectors actually behaved would have meant designing a progression system in the dark.

You have to earn the right to gate things. Campaigns build the track record that makes tiers meaningful.

Execution

The hardest part wasn't the product. It was the factory.

QR authentication is straightforward until you try to integrate it into existing manufacturing workflows. Factories optimise for throughput and consistency. Adding a per-unit unique identifier — with placement constraints, visual specs, and database linkage — disrupted that flow in ways that weren't visible from a product spec.

The lesson I carry from this: factory QC requirements need to be in scope from Phase 1, not retrofitted in Phase 3. We designed placement standards early but left inspection guidelines too late. When the first production batches came back with inconsistencies, we had to redesign the QC process mid-cycle.

Spec QC requirements in Phase 1. Every week you wait is a production batch you can't redo.

Sticker placement standards

Defined visual and physical placement specs per product line. Balanced aesthetic integration with scan reliability — particularly for display-only collectors who never open packaging. Required close collaboration with brand and industrial design teams.

Factory QC integration

Designed inspection guidelines for factories with no prior experience with digital product identity. Involved production leads earlier than planned after first-batch issues surfaced. The result was a repeatable QC process that scaled across product lines.

Authentication database sync

Every production batch had to link to the authentication database before reaching retail. Designed the batch ingestion flow and worked with engineering to build monitoring for sync failures — a critical dependency that had no fallback if it broke at launch.

Operations monitoring dashboard

Internal tool for tracking scan activity, authentication errors, and production batch status. Gave ops and support real-time visibility into system health — and gave us early signal on collector behaviour patterns before Vault campaigns launched.

Reflection

What I know now

Learning

Spec QC requirements in Phase 1, not Phase 3

We defined sticker placement standards early but left factory inspection guidelines too late. When the first production batches came back with placement inconsistencies, we had to redesign the QC process while production was already moving. Earlier factory workshops — before any spec was written — would have surfaced the throughput constraints that caused the problems.

Learning

First-scan onboarding is a product problem, not a marketing one

Scan adoption didn't happen automatically. Many collectors weren't aware Vault existed after unboxing. We treated first-scan awareness as a marketing channel problem when it's actually a product moment: the 30 seconds after unboxing. I'd design the first-scan experience into the product spec from day one — what happens immediately after the first scan is the most important UX decision in the entire system.

Next

Always-on engagement between campaigns

Campaigns drive spikes. Between launches, active collector sessions drop. The platform needs passive engagement mechanics — collection display, collector identity features, social proof — that sustain interest without a campaign being live. The current model is too event-dependent to build a habit.

Next

Secondary market ownership transfer

Collectors trade heavily. Vault currently reflects first scan only, not current ownership. Solving transfer would unlock platform utility for secondary market collectors and create a stronger trust signal for the authentication system. It's also the feature most likely to make Vault genuinely useful across the full collector lifecycle.
