Context

Why a collectibles company moved into interactive toys

Mighty Jaxx built a strong business around collectible designer figures, but collectibles are a one-time purchase. Once a figure goes on a shelf, the interaction ends.

IP partners wanted products that extended fan engagement beyond purchase, and the company had characters with storytelling potential sitting idle. That opened a new category: physical collectibles that unlock interactive storytelling and conversation, without a screen.

"Place a figurine on the base. The character wakes up and starts a conversation. No screen, no buttons, no setup. Just a toy that becomes a companion."

I was the primary PM, responsible for defining the product experience from the ground up: how the ecosystem's pieces connect, how interactions work without a screen, and how content scales across characters and environments. My work spanned interaction design, content ecosystem structure, and hardware-software behaviour definition, coordinating across hardware vendors, engineering, content creators, and licensing partners.

The Audience

Designing for two decision-makers with different goals

Children experience the product; parents buy it. Designing for both required navigating their different priorities, a tension that shaped every major product decision.

Primary user

Children (3–10)

Characters they love: familiar IP or original personalities they can build a relationship with

Stories that respond to them, not just play at them: a sense of agency in the narrative

Interaction that feels immediate and magical: placing a toy and having it talk back

New content and surprises over time, with a reason to keep coming back

Purchase decision-maker

Parents

Screen-free: the product should not become another reason for a child to stare at a display

Safe and age-appropriate, especially for a product that talks directly with their child

Educational or imaginative value, not passive consumption

Clear control: visibility into what the product says and does, with parental oversight

Ecosystem Design

One experience, four connected components

A standalone speaker lacks physical interaction; physical toys alone lack storytelling. The product had to be an ecosystem: hardware, content, and software as a unified experience.

🔊 Speaker Base Central interaction hub · NFC detection · Voice I/O · LED feedback
🧸 Character Figurines NFC-enabled · Place on base to activate · ~60 min of base content per character
☁️ Content System Stories · Songs · Interactive scenarios · Conversational AI · Expandable over time
📱 Parent App Content controls · Usage visibility · Safe conversation monitoring

The base is the physical hub. Figurines trigger experiences via NFC. Content lives in the cloud. The parent app provides oversight and control.

🧸

Figurines

Each character has an NFC tag and ~60 minutes of base content: stories, songs, and interactive experiences, with a distinct personality shaping conversational responses.

🏡

Playsets

Environment-based accessories (forest, home, school) that introduce new activities. Any character can use a playset, but certain pairings unlock bonus content, creating cross-sell incentive.

🔊

Speaker Base

Manages NFC detection, voice I/O, LED cues, and mode transitions. No screen.

☁️

Content System

Pre-loaded stories work offline; branching narratives and conversational mode require a connection. New characters, playsets, and content expand the system over time.

Product Decisions

Three decisions that shaped the system

Each involved real constraints and competing pressures.

01

Committing to screen-free, even when a screen would have been easier

The temptation

Early concepts included a small display on the base. A screen would have made it easier to show animations, guide children through prompts, and give demos something visible. Internal teams liked the option.

Why we removed it

A screen puts the product in direct competition with tablets, collapsing the value proposition. Without it, the interaction simplified: place a character, respond to a voice.

02

NFC as the physical trigger: choosing simplicity over capability

Alternatives considered

Bluetooth required pairing and batteries in each figurine. Camera recognition raised privacy concerns and had lighting reliability issues.

Why NFC

NFC identifies a figurine the instant it touches the base: no pairing, no batteries in the toy. Place the character, it activates. Any setup step breaks the interaction for a 3–10 year old.

03

How open should character conversations be? A real design debate.

Option A: Fully open

Characters respond to almost any question within safety limits. Natural, but conversations drift outside the character's universe, removing the incentive to buy new playsets.

Option B: Scene-locked

Conversations are tightly constrained by the active playset. Children become frustrated by constant redirects, and the interaction feels scripted rather than alive.

What we chose: Hybrid

Characters respond broadly, but answers are shaped by the active character, environment, and safety layer. Off-scene questions get acknowledged, then redirected: "That's interesting! But since we're in the forest today, want to explore the river with me?"

Designing Under Constraints

The product is defined by what it can't do

Screen-free interaction means the system communicates only through voice, sound, and light. Hardware minimalism means a single NFC sensor, microphones, and LED indicators. Designing within those limits was the brief.

🚫
No screen

Navigation became placement. Menus became voice prompts. Visual feedback became LED cues and sound design.

📡
Single NFC sensor, limited inputs

One figurine at a time, no physical buttons beyond power. The entire interaction model is built around placement and voice: the two most natural inputs for young children.

🧒
Children's unpredictable responses

Children pause, repeat phrases, answer unrelated questions, go silent, or talk over the character. The system handles all of these with fallback behaviours that keep stories moving without making the child feel like they did something wrong.

🔒
Licensed IP character integrity

Licensed characters have tightly controlled tone, vocabulary, and worldview. A conversational system that generates flexible responses is in direct tension with that, and a single out-of-character response in a live partner demo could end a licensing relationship. A policy layer, character personality guidelines, and I/O filtering are built into the system from the start.

🛡️
Safety requirements for direct child interaction

Input filtering, output validation, and conversation boundaries enforced by a policy engine — all before any response reaches the child.

Pre-Launch Validation

Validating interaction assumptions before the full build

With the product still in development, research focused on the core interaction assumptions that had to be true for the ecosystem to work at all.

01

Will children understand the interaction model without instruction?

If children couldn't figure out that placing a figurine activates the character, nothing else would work.

Signals we looked for:

  • Children attempting to place the figurine without being told to
  • Understanding that the character was now "active" and listening
  • Responding to voice prompts without needing to be coached
02

How do children actually behave during voice interaction?

The system needed to be designed for real child behaviour, not idealised responses.

Patterns observed:

  • Long pauses before responding, often 3–5 seconds or longer
  • Repeating the prompt back instead of answering
  • Responding with unrelated phrases or questions
  • Talking over the character mid-sentence

These patterns informed the fallback response design: stories continue regardless of how the child responds.

03

Do children form emotional connection with character voices?

Without emotional connection to the character, there's no reason to return.

Signals we looked for:

  • Continuing conversations beyond the first prompt unprompted
  • Asking questions back to the character
  • Replaying story segments voluntarily
  • Referring to the character by name in conversation

Metrics planned for launch

Interaction quality Story completion rate Whether children follow interactive stories to completion
Voice engagement Response participation rate Whether children actively respond to prompts or passively listen
Companion behaviour Conversation duration Average time in conversational mode; proxy for companion vs. toy experience
Retention signal Replay rate How often children return to completed stories; signals lasting content value
Cross-Functional Execution

Four disciplines that had to move together

Hardware, software, content, and licensing each owned a critical piece and none could proceed independently.

Hardware

Translating product requirements into device behaviour

Hardware was built by external vendors; software was managed internally. Bridging both required defining behaviours specifically enough for vendors to implement without constraining engineering. Key areas: NFC detection timing for small children's hands, microphone calibration for children's voices at varying distances, and speaker volume safe for young ears.

Content

Designing a scalable storytelling pipeline

Interactive storytelling spans branching narratives, activity scripts, fallback responses, personality guidelines, and voice briefs, all of which interact. I defined a framework covering timing windows, branching logic, and fallback coverage so content production could scale without a product review on every piece.

Licensing

Designing for character integrity without killing conversational flexibility

Conversational AI generates flexible responses that can easily drift out of a licensed character's established vocabulary and tone. I worked with licensing partners to define personality layers that constrained the system without making conversations feel scripted.

Partner Demos

Designing demonstrations for licensing and retail partners

A live demo must work reliably with a stranger operating it under time pressure. I designed curated flows showcasing the core loop: place character, activate story, enter conversation. The demo was itself a product design problem.

What I owned
Interaction design

Mapped how children trigger experiences, navigate between modes, and how the system responds to unpredictable inputs. Defined the interaction flows hardware vendors and engineering built against.

Content ecosystem structure

Defined what each character and playset unlocks, how content expands over time, and the framework content creators worked within.

Hardware-software behaviour definition

Translated product requirements into specifications for external vendors: NFC detection logic, microphone timing, LED feedback states, audio output behaviour.

Partner demonstration design

Designed and prepared demonstration flows for licensors and retail partners: reliable enough for live environments, clear enough to communicate the ecosystem vision.

Reflection

What this project taught me about 0→1 product work

Learning

Physical interaction affordances need to be self-explanatory

The figurine placement model works because it mirrors natural play behaviour. Any interaction that required explanation would have been abandoned.

Learning

Voice systems must be designed for the user's actual behaviour, not ideal behaviour

Designing fallback responses was a core product requirement, not a polish task. Every story beat needed a graceful continuation path for silence, repetition, and unrelated answers.

Learning

Licensing constraints are product constraints, not external blockers

Character personality layers, conversation boundaries, and content review processes are built into the system, not added later.

Open question

How much of the ecosystem value survives without the content pipeline?

The hardware interaction model is strong. Long-term value depends on a continuous content pipeline, which is a separate operational capability the company still needs to build. The open question isn't about the product; it's whether content infrastructure can scale fast enough to sustain engagement past the first few characters.
