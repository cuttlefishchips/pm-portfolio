01 — The Problem

Granola stops at recall.
The real value is what happens after.

Granola has solved the hardest part of meetings: capture. But its value loop ends there. Commitments made across meetings stay buried in individual notes, organized by when the meeting happened — not by what still needs doing.

Open Items fills this

For users in 4–7 meetings daily, the cognitive load of tracking personal commitments across a growing note library is high enough that they maintain a parallel task system outside Granola — always slightly out of date, always one more thing to maintain.

Granola’s co-founder has stated the ambition: “It is not about just capturing a meeting, but a space where you can work, even post meetings.” Open Items is the first concrete step toward that.

02 — Key Decisions

The choices that shaped the feature

Three decisions where I explicitly chose a direction and rejected an alternative. The reasoning is the work.

01
Precision over recall in extraction — conservative by default

The highest-risk moment is the first impression. If the first list a user sees contains items that are not their commitments, they conclude the feature is broken and never return. Better to surface 6 high-confidence items than 12 that include noise. Recall improves iteratively. First impressions do not.

Rejected Broad extraction with user-side filtering — puts the burden on users and confirms the feature is untrustworthy before trust is established.
02
60-day full trial, then paid only — no permanent free tier

Two failure modes were evaluated: a permanent free tier (subsidizes non-converters, spends the pricing lever prematurely) and a capped preview (creates friction at peak engagement). A full trial avoids both. The user accumulates 60 days of evidence the feature is load-bearing before facing a conversion decision with real stakes. Trial expiry is framed around specific items and history at risk — not feature marketing.

Rejected Permanent free tier with caps — adversarial conversion moment, eliminates future pricing flexibility, subsidizes non-converting users at scale.
03
The Anti-Jira Principle: Open Items must never require user input to function

Smart grouping, priority inference, custom labels — all valuable in Phase 2. But every addition must be additive, never required. If a user never touches a configuration option, Open Items must still be fully useful. The moment users are filling in fields to make the list work, the product has become a worse version of every task manager they already use.

Rejected Mandatory priority fields, required labelling, AI-inferred due dates — all violate the principle and erode what makes this distinctly Granola.
03 — The Solution

Open Items

A native Granola view that automatically extracts personal commitments from meeting notes and surfaces them in a single consolidated list — with source context, across all meetings, in one place. No setup. No configuration.

Vision: Transform Granola from a place where your meetings live into a system that ensures your meetings actually lead to something.

Each item carries the action text, a context snippet from the transcript (enough to act without reopening the note), the source meeting and date, and a status: Open, Done, or Dismissed. Done and Dismissed are intentionally distinct — completing a task and deciding it is no longer relevant are different signals, and conflating them degrades list quality over time.

04 — Success Metrics

Explicitly Not Measuring

Completion rate within a defined timeframe. A 2-hour action and a 2-week follow-up cannot be held to the same window — a fixed timeframe creates false precision and incentivizes optimizing extraction toward short-horizon items. List trust is the right proxy for value.

05 — Tradeoffs

What we gave up and why

Two tradeoffs significant enough to name explicitly. The full list is in the PRD.

Feature gravity locks the pricing decision

Once Open Items is included in the paid plan, it is almost impossible to move it to a higher tier later. The window to treat it as a premium differentiator is at launch — after that it becomes table stakes. This means the MVP pricing call is effectively permanent. That is an acceptable cost for the retention mechanics it enables, but it needs to be a conscious decision, not a default.

Extraction quality is the only dependency that can kill the feature on day one

Every other risk is recoverable. Noisy extraction at first impression is not. If the first list a user sees does not feel accurate, the negative impression is almost impossible to reverse at scale. This means the extraction prompt must be validated with human-rated quality checks before launch — not just automated metrics. The cost is time. The cost of skipping it is a failed feature.
